The Situation

LGT APAC's technology delivery operates within Group IT's SAFe framework. The framework was designed to bring coordination and predictability to large-scale technology programmes. In the version implemented at LGT, the coordination overhead has grown faster than the value it coordinates. SAFe was designed for organisations with hundreds of interdependent teams. Below approximately 150 people in a delivery organisation, the ceremony-to-output ratio typically inverts — the framework designed to reduce friction becomes its primary source. Teams spend significant capacity in planning ceremonies, roadmap synchronisation, and governance reporting — time that comes directly out of delivery.

This is not a critique of the framework's intent. It is an observation about fit and timing.

The business consequence is visible: time from approved idea to production is long; approval layers between a business need and a working solution are numerous; and accountability for outcomes is distributed across enough roles that no single person owns the result when something underperforms. The Product Owner owns the backlog. The Product Manager owns the roadmap. The Business Owner owns value. The RTE owns delivery. When a product underperforms, each role has a defensible explanation. Distributed accountability is structurally indistinguishable from no accountability. When the product fails to perform, everyone is partially responsible — which means, in practice, no one is.

A second pressure has arrived, sharper and more urgent. AI-assisted development tools have materially changed what a small, focused team can produce. The AI cost multiplier. If AI tools double a team's effective delivery output, but governance still processes at the same rate, the bottleneck doubles in impact. The cost of slow PI gate reviews was tolerable when it was the primary constraint. It is not tolerable when delivery capacity has lapped governance throughput. The mismatch will widen as AI capability continues to improve. When delivery capacity increases and governance throughput does not, the governance bottleneck becomes the binding constraint. The current model was not designed for this environment.

What We're Proposing

A bounded pilot: one product, one team, six months, operating under a different delivery model. Three structural changes.

1. Single-Threaded Ownership

Each product has one named owner — not a Product Owner plus a Product Manager plus a Business Owner as separate roles, but one person who holds the intent, resolves priority conflicts, and is accountable for whether the product succeeds in the market. Amazon formalised this as the Single-Threaded Owner (STO). The STO is not split across initiatives — not one primary thing plus secondary responsibilities, but genuinely one thing. The moment an owner is split, they become a coordinator. The current model already produces too many coordinators. If the product underperforms, this person is responsible. If it succeeds, they receive the credit.

The STO model is more viable today than when Amazon introduced it. AI and the STO. AI coding assistants, analytical tools, and automated testing have reduced the minimum viable team size for meaningful product work. An STO with AI tools can today own a product surface that previously required a larger team and a separate PM layer. The "two-pizza team" principle now operates with smaller teams. The leverage available to a focused owner has increased materially — making the case for removing the coordination layer above them stronger, not weaker. AI tools have reduced the minimum viable headcount for meaningful product work. An STO with AI assistance can own what previously required a larger team and a dedicated product management layer sitting above it.

2. Outcome Metrics, Not Process Metrics

The pilot team is measured on three things agreed upfront: time from approved idea to production; defect escape rate; and one business outcome metric specific to the product. Not PI predictability. Not velocity. Not ceremony attendance. A team that ships valuable software on time has done its job. A team that hits 94% PI predictability while shipping software nobody uses has not.

3. Governance by Embedding, Not by Queue

Compliance, risk, and security reviews remain mandatory. They move to the front of the process — design review, not release gate. The pilot team has an embedded compliance contact who flags issues early rather than reviewing late. Escalation to Group IT governance happens for exceptions, not for routine releases. AI and embedded governance. AI introduces new governance categories — model explainability, training data lineage, bias monitoring, and MAS model risk guidelines — that a generalist SAFe ceremony queue cannot assess correctly. An embedded compliance contact develops this expertise over time. A shared review queue, staffed by generalists rotating across teams, cannot. For AI products especially, governance by embedding is not a convenience — it is the only model that works. This model does not reduce governance. It moves governance to where it produces the least friction and the most impact.

The AI Inflection

The proposal's timing is not incidental. The arrival of capable AI in software delivery has changed two structural facts simultaneously — and the current model is ill-suited to either.

Opportunity: AI multiplies the leverage of small teams

AI-assisted coding, automated testing, and analytical synthesis have increased the effective output of a small, focused team. The coordination overhead of SAFe was partly justified by the need to synchronise large numbers of people. As capable teams become smaller, that justification weakens. Organisations that have adopted AI-augmented delivery — and matched it with a low-overhead delivery model — are showing material improvements in time-to-production without proportional increases in headcount.

The relevant competitive benchmark for LGT APAC is not other large banks still running their own version of this process. It is leaner competitors who are shipping AI-assisted client tools, onboarding automation, and portfolio analytics at a pace the current model structurally cannot match.

Opportunity: AI-native product ownership

A single-threaded owner can hold the full AI product lifecycle — training data quality, model behaviour in production, business metric correlation, and regulatory posture — in a way a distributed role structure cannot. AI products drift. Models degrade. The feedback loop between model behaviour and business outcome requires someone watching both simultaneously. An STO accountable for the outcome is the correct organisational unit for this. A Product Owner watching the backlog and a separate Business Owner watching the P&L are not.

Risk: AI creates governance obligations the current model cannot meet

AI model deployments do not fit quarterly PI release cycles. Models require continuous evaluation, retraining triggers, and production monitoring. MAS guidelines on model risk governance — relevant for any AI deployed in Singapore — require ongoing oversight that a point-in-time release gate review cannot satisfy. An AI feature that passes a SAFe release gate can still fail its regulatory obligations in production three months later.

The embedded compliance model described in this proposal is better suited to AI governance than queue-based review. An embedded contact who understands a specific product's model and data can monitor it continuously. A centralised review team rotating across products cannot build the contextual expertise that AI governance requires.

Risk: Platform control over AI becomes a competitive bottleneck

If AI tooling is managed as a Group IT shared platform service, APAC's ability to adopt AI for client-facing products depends on Group IT's roadmap priorities. This concentrates control over a rapidly-moving competitive differentiator in a function whose priorities may not reflect APAC's client-facing urgency. The competitive window for AI-assisted private banking features is measured in months. A shared-resource model with Group-level prioritisation is structurally unable to respond at that speed.

Why a Pilot, Not a Transformation

We are not proposing to exit SAFe for APAC technology. We are proposing to demonstrate, with a single team over six months, that outcome-led delivery produces better results within LGT's governance requirements. If it does, the evidence supports a broader case to Group IT. If it does not, the cost is contained and we revert.

The ask to Group IT is narrow and deliberately so. Each of these three asks addresses a specific structural blocker without requiring Group IT to revise the framework for other teams. The pilot is designed to be easy to grant, easy to observe, and easy to evaluate. The narrowness is intentional: a broader ask invites a broader refusal. The pilot team's governance obligations are satisfied through the embedded compliance model; PI planning ceremony requirements are suspended for the duration; and the team retains access to shared infrastructure as-is.

Pilot Candidate

To be identified: A product or team in APAC that meets three criteria — (a) relatively low interdependency with other ARTs; (b) a clear, measurable business outcome metric; (c) a product person capable and willing to operate as a single-threaded owner. Avoid products with heavy Group-level integration. A client-facing digital product or internal platform capability with contained scope is ideal. An AI-adjacent product would make the pilot doubly relevant.

The Broader Context

The banking industry is not unique in discovering that coordination frameworks can become the bottleneck they were designed to prevent. The organisations that have moved past this share a common structural shift: they stopped measuring teams on process adherence and started measuring them on outcomes. They pushed decision authority to the team with the most context, not the team highest in the governance hierarchy.

Amazon's Single-Threaded Owner. Mission command in modern military organisations. Outcomes-based accreditation in healthcare. Different domains, the same escape route: In each case, the escape required changing two things simultaneously — what was being measured (outcomes, not compliance) and where authority lived (execution level, not governance level). Changing only one produces a hybrid that satisfies neither model and usually reverts to the old one within a year. stop synchronising process; start measuring outcomes. Push authority to the team that holds the most context.

APAC has the scale to run this pilot without disrupting Group-level programmes. It also has the business pressure — from clients, from competitors, from an AI landscape that is not waiting — to move faster than the current model permits.

The downside of running this pilot is six months of careful observation. The downside of not running it is another year of the same, in an environment that is not standing still.
